Local-vol calibration via nonlinear least squares

This note collects the full set of changes needed to switch calibrate_lv_bysections from scipy.optimize.minimize(SLSQP) on a scalar RMSE to scipy.optimize.least_squares on a residual vector, and explains the mathematics of why this is the right choice for a calibration objective.

1. Why this change matters

Your current setup minimises a single scalar:

$$ f(\boldsymbol{\theta}) \;=\; \sqrt{\tfrac{1}{m}\sum_{i=1}^{m}\bigl(\text{pde}_i(\boldsymbol{\theta}) - \text{cf}_i\bigr)^2} $$

with $\boldsymbol{\theta}\in\mathbb{R}^n$ (here $n=6$ for BiExp) and $m$ strikes per expiry. SLSQP receives this single number, computes a finite-difference gradient $\nabla f \in \mathbb{R}^n$ (costing $n{+}1$ extra objective evaluations per step), and proceeds by line search.

This throws away an essential piece of structure: the objective is quadratic in residuals. Reformulating it as

$$ \boldsymbol{r}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) \;=\; \bigl(\text{pde}_i(\boldsymbol{\theta}) - \text{cf}_i\bigr)_{i=1\ldots m} \;\in\;\mathbb{R}^m, \qquad \min_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} \; \tfrac{1}{2}\,\|\boldsymbol{r}(\boldsymbol{\theta})\|^2 $$

lets us use specialised nonlinear least squares methods (Levenberg–Marquardt / trust-region reflective) that exploit this structure to converge in roughly 3–10× fewer PDE evaluations.

2. From linear regression to nonlinear least squares

2.1 The case you already know: linear regression

Classical least-squares regression fits a model that is linear in its parameters:

$$ y_i \approx \boldsymbol{\phi}(x_i)^{\!\top}\boldsymbol{\beta}, \qquad i=1,\ldots,m $$

where $\boldsymbol{\phi}(x_i)\in\mathbb{R}^n$ is a vector of basis-function evaluations and $\boldsymbol{\beta}\in\mathbb{R}^n$ are parameters. Stacking the rows $\boldsymbol{\phi}(x_i)^{\!\top}$ into a design matrix $\boldsymbol{X}\in\mathbb{R}^{m\times n}$:

$$ \min_{\boldsymbol{\beta}} \;\tfrac{1}{2}\,\|\boldsymbol{y} - \boldsymbol{X}\boldsymbol{\beta}\|^2 \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; \boldsymbol{X}^{\!\top}\boldsymbol{X}\,\boldsymbol{\beta}^{*} = \boldsymbol{X}^{\!\top}\boldsymbol{y} $$

This is closed-form: one linear solve, done. The reason it works is that the residual $\boldsymbol{r}(\boldsymbol{\beta}) = \boldsymbol{y} - \boldsymbol{X}\boldsymbol{\beta}$ depends linearly on the parameters.

2.2 Your case: nonlinear in the parameters

In your problem each residual is the output of a forward PDE for given LV parameters $\boldsymbol{\theta}$:

$$ r_i(\boldsymbol{\theta}) \;=\; \text{pde}_i(\boldsymbol{\theta}) - \text{cf}_i. $$

There is no design matrix. $\text{pde}_i$ is a highly nonlinear functional of $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ (LV section $\to$ PDE coefficients $\to$ density evolution $\to$ expectation). The closed-form normal equations no longer apply.

2.3 The key idea: linearise locally, iterate

Even though $\boldsymbol{r}$ is nonlinear, near any iterate $\boldsymbol{\theta}_k$ we can take a first-order Taylor expansion:

$$ \boldsymbol{r}(\boldsymbol{\theta}_k + \boldsymbol{\delta}) \;\approx\; \boldsymbol{r}(\boldsymbol{\theta}_k) + \boldsymbol{J}_k\,\boldsymbol{\delta}, \qquad \boldsymbol{J}_k \;=\; \frac{\partial \boldsymbol{r}}{\partial \boldsymbol{\theta}}\bigg|_{\boldsymbol{\theta}_k} \;\in\;\mathbb{R}^{m\times n}. $$

Substituting this approximation into the objective gives a linear least-squares problem in the step $\boldsymbol{\delta}$:

$$ \min_{\boldsymbol{\delta}} \;\tfrac{1}{2}\,\|\boldsymbol{r}(\boldsymbol{\theta}_k) + \boldsymbol{J}_k\,\boldsymbol{\delta}\|^2 \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; \boldsymbol{J}_k^{\!\top}\boldsymbol{J}_k\,\boldsymbol{\delta} \;=\; -\,\boldsymbol{J}_k^{\!\top}\boldsymbol{r}(\boldsymbol{\theta}_k). $$

You solve this small linear system, update $\boldsymbol{\theta}_{k+1} = \boldsymbol{\theta}_k + \boldsymbol{\delta}$, re-linearise, and repeat. This is the Gauss–Newton method.

One-line summary. Nonlinear least squares = a sequence of linear least squares problems on the local linearisation of the residual. The "regression" you know is doing the same thing — just once, because for linear $\boldsymbol{r}$ the linearisation is exact.

2.4 Why Gauss–Newton beats general gradient descent

Consider the gradient and Hessian of $f(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \tfrac{1}{2}\|\boldsymbol{r}(\boldsymbol{\theta})\|^2$:

$$ \nabla f \;=\; \boldsymbol{J}^{\!\top}\boldsymbol{r}, \qquad \nabla^2 f \;=\; \underbrace{\boldsymbol{J}^{\!\top}\boldsymbol{J}}_{\text{cheap, p.s.d.}} \;+\; \underbrace{\sum_{i=1}^{m} r_i\,\nabla^2 r_i}_{\text{expensive, small near optimum}}. $$

The first piece $\boldsymbol{J}^{\!\top}\boldsymbol{J}$ is free once you have $\boldsymbol{J}$, and it is positive semi-definite. The second piece involves second derivatives of each residual but is multiplied by $r_i$, which become small near a good fit. Gauss–Newton just drops it:

$$ \boldsymbol{H}_{\text{GN}} \;:=\; \boldsymbol{J}^{\!\top}\boldsymbol{J} \;\approx\; \nabla^2 f. $$

Compared to a generic minimiser of $f$:

  • Hessian for free. Generic methods either don't use a Hessian (Nelder–Mead, Powell) or pay $O(n^2)$ extra evaluations to estimate it (BFGS builds it up; finite-difference Hessian is quadratic). Gauss–Newton's Hessian approximation costs you nothing extra.
  • Better information from the Jacobian. Finite-differencing $\boldsymbol{r}$ to get $\boldsymbol{J} \in \mathbb{R}^{m\times n}$ costs the same $n{+}1$ evaluations as finite-differencing the scalar $f$ to get $\nabla f \in \mathbb{R}^n$ — but you get $m$ times more numerical information per call.
  • Quadratic convergence near the optimum. When residuals are small, Gauss–Newton's Hessian approximation becomes accurate and the method converges quadratically — just like full Newton, but without the Hessian cost.

2.5 Levenberg–Marquardt: making it robust far from the optimum

The bare Gauss–Newton step

$$ \boldsymbol{\delta}_{\text{GN}} \;=\; -(\boldsymbol{J}^{\!\top}\boldsymbol{J})^{-1}\boldsymbol{J}^{\!\top}\boldsymbol{r} $$

can be far too aggressive when $\boldsymbol{J}^{\!\top}\boldsymbol{J}$ is ill-conditioned or when $\boldsymbol{\theta}_k$ is far from a minimum. Levenberg–Marquardt adds a damping term:

$$ (\boldsymbol{J}^{\!\top}\boldsymbol{J} + \lambda\,\boldsymbol{D})\,\boldsymbol{\delta} \;=\; -\,\boldsymbol{J}^{\!\top}\boldsymbol{r} $$

where $\boldsymbol{D}$ is a positive-definite scaling matrix (often $\boldsymbol{I}$ or $\text{diag}(\boldsymbol{J}^{\!\top}\boldsymbol{J})$) and $\lambda\ge 0$ is adapted each iteration:

  • $\lambda \to 0$: step approaches Gauss–Newton (fast, possibly wild).
  • $\lambda \to \infty$: step approaches scaled steepest descent $-\frac{1}{\lambda}\boldsymbol{D}^{-1}\boldsymbol{J}^{\!\top}\boldsymbol{r}$ (slow but safe).

The algorithm increases $\lambda$ when a trial step worsens the objective (retreat toward steepest descent), and decreases it when steps succeed (advance toward Gauss–Newton). This is what scipy.optimize.least_squares does internally when you pass method="lm".

2.6 Trust-region reflective: bounds for free

Levenberg–Marquardt is unbounded. Your LV parameters live in a box, so you need a bounded variant. method="trf" (trust-region reflective) keeps the Gauss–Newton spirit but instead of $\lambda$-damping it constrains each step to a trust region $\|\boldsymbol{\delta}\|\le \Delta_k$ that shrinks/grows based on whether the linearised model accurately predicted the actual residual reduction.

Bounds are enforced by reflection: if a proposed step would cross a bound at face $\theta_j = \text{lb}_j$, the component $\delta_j$ is reflected back into the feasible region, and the Jacobian column $\boldsymbol{J}_{:,j}$ is rescaled so the optimizer doesn't keep proposing infeasible directions there. All iterates $\boldsymbol{\theta}_k$ stay strictly inside the box.

MethodBoundsNotes
"trf"yesTrust-region + reflection. Your default for this problem.

3. Why this beats minimize(rmse, SLSQP) on your problem

minimize(rmse, SLSQP) least_squares(residuals, trf)
Gradient $\nabla f \in \mathbb{R}^n$ from finite differences ($n{+}1$ evals). $\boldsymbol{J} \in \mathbb{R}^{m\times n}$ from finite differences ($n{+}1$ evals). Same cost, far more information.
Hessian BFGS approximation built over many iterations. $\boldsymbol{J}^{\!\top}\boldsymbol{J}$ for free, accurate near optimum.
Convergence near minimum Linear (quasi-Newton). Quadratic.
Bounds Active-set, can chatter. Reflection, always feasible.
Typical PDE-eval count Hundreds. Tens.

4. Code changes

4.1 Refactor LvObjectiveBuilder

Inside lvsection_calib.py, extract a private helper that performs the single expensive operation (one PDE step + price evaluation) and have both objective and the new residuals route through it. This avoids duplicating the PDE code.

def _pde_prices(self, params):
    """Run the PDE for given params and return the PDE prices vector.
       Returns None if check_params rejects the parameters."""
    self.lv.update_params(self.exp_idx, params)
    is_ok, _ = self.lv.check_params(self.exp_idx)
    if not is_ok:
        return None

    x, dx, p = fpde.density_step(self.old_p, self.old_x, self.old_dx,
                                 self.step_grid, self.lv, self.pde_config)
    self.new_x, self.new_p, self.new_dx = x, p, dx

    s = self.fwd * np.exp(x)
    prices = []
    for k in self.strikes:
        match self.option_type:
            case OptionType.CALL:     payoff = np.maximum(s - k, 0.0)
            case OptionType.PUT:      payoff = np.maximum(k - s, 0.0)
            case OptionType.STRADDLE: payoff = np.abs(s - k)
            case _:
                raise ValueError(f"Unsupported option type: {self.option_type}")
        prices.append(np.trapezoid(payoff * p, x))

    prices = np.asarray(prices)
    self.pde_prices = prices
    self.rmse = metrics.rmse(prices, self.cf_prices)
    return prices


def objective(self, params):
    """Scalar RMSE objective (kept for back-compat with minimize-based code)."""
    prices = self._pde_prices(params)
    if prices is None:
        return self.cf_prices.sum()
    return self.rmse


def residuals(self, params):
    """Residual vector (pde - cf) for scipy.optimize.least_squares."""
    prices = self._pde_prices(params)
    if prices is None:
        # Vector-valued penalty: large ||r||^2 steers the trust region away.
        return np.full_like(self.cf_prices, np.sqrt(self.cf_prices.sum()))
    return prices - np.asarray(self.cf_prices)

4.2 Update the calibration loop

Inside calibrate_lv_bysections, replace the optimizer call in the per-expiry loop:

from scipy.optimize import least_squares

# ... inside the for exp_idx in range(...) loop ...

bounds = lv.section(exp_idx).constraints()       # a scipy Bounds object

result = least_squares(
    obj_builder.residuals,
    x0=params_init,
    bounds=(bounds.lb, bounds.ub),               # least_squares wants a 2-tuple
    method="trf",                              # trust-region reflective; supports bounds
    xtol=tol,
    ftol=ftols[exp_idx],
    # max_nfev=200,                              # optional safety cap
)
sol = result.x

# Sync the LV with the final solution and refresh cached density / rmse
lv.update_params(exp_idx, sol)
rmse = obj_builder.objective(sol)

4.3 What about the existing create_optimizer factory?

For now, bypass it for the least-squares path — the cleanest minimum-change edit. Once you've verified the speedup on your real calibration set, you can extend create_optimizer in optimization.py to recognise "lm" / "trf" and route them to a wrapper whose .minimize(residuals_fn, x0, bounds) calls least_squares under the hood. That preserves the existing config-driven optimizer selection.

5. Practical notes

5.1 Bounds carry over cleanly

  • Your existing Bounds(lw_bounds, up_bounds) from the BiExp model transfers verbatim — just unpack it as (bounds.lb, bounds.ub).
  • "trf" keeps every iterate strictly inside the box via reflection. "lm" doesn't support bounds and will raise an error if you pass them.

5.2 check_params penalty still works

The "invalid parameters ⇒ large penalty" trick translates directly: return a constant vector of large values instead of a large scalar. Trust-region reflective sees the large $\|\boldsymbol{r}\|^2$, shrinks the trust region, and steers the next iterate away from the offending region.

5.3 Tolerances

  • ftol: stop when relative reduction in $\|\boldsymbol{r}\|^2$ falls below this. Set it to the price-noise floor of your PDE (likely $10^{-5}$ to $10^{-4}$ in normalised units). No point converging tighter than your prices are accurate.
  • xtol: stop when relative change in $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ falls below this. Typically the same order as ftol.
  • gtol (default $10^{-8}$): first-order optimality test on $\boldsymbol{J}^{\!\top}\boldsymbol{r}$. Rarely the binding criterion in practice.

5.4 Don't supply jac=

Unless you want to derive analytical $\partial \text{pde}_i / \partial \theta_j$ through your PDE (hard), let least_squares finite-difference it. The Jacobian is computed in $n{+}1$ PDE calls per iteration — the same cost as finite-differencing the scalar objective with SLSQP, but the resulting linear algebra extracts far more information.

5.5 Validation

Before fully replacing SLSQP in production, run both side-by-side on one expiry and check that:

  1. final $\|\boldsymbol{r}\|$ is comparable or better;
  2. final $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ is similar (or, if different, gives equivalent market fit — calibration objectives often have flat optima);
  3. number of PDE evaluations (result.nfev) is significantly lower.

6. Recap

  • Mathematically. Nonlinear least squares solves $\min \tfrac{1}{2}\|\boldsymbol{r}(\boldsymbol{\theta})\|^2$ as a sequence of linear least-squares subproblems on the local linearisation of $\boldsymbol{r}$. This is Gauss–Newton; LM and TRF are robustified variants. The "Hessian-for-free" property $\boldsymbol{H}\approx\boldsymbol{J}^{\!\top}\boldsymbol{J}$ is what makes it so much more efficient than a generic scalar minimiser on the same problem.
  • Practically. Add a residuals method to LvObjectiveBuilder returning the per-strike price differences, and call scipy.optimize.least_squares(..., method="trf", bounds=(lb, ub)) in the calibration loop. Your bounds, your check_params penalty, and your existing per-expiry density passing all stay unchanged.
